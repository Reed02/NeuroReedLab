Simulation Basis

Aspen Plus model overview

The Aspen simulation started from the theoretical PFD and went through a lot of iterations before it finally converged and produced product. Since the simulation was constantly being tweaked throughout development, all the material balance, energy balance, and economic numbers are based on the theoretical PFD rather than the Aspen model, just to have a stable reference point.

The final simulation produces approximately 56,355 kg/hr of ε-caprolactam, which works out to about 544,000 US tons/year, comfortably above the 500,000 ton/year target.

Model Assumptions

Key assumptions enabling convergence

Vapor-phase phenol feed

Phenol was assumed to enter R-1 as a vapor instead of a liquid, turning it into a vapor-vapor reaction and letting the PFR kinetics converge in Aspen.

Liquid-phase hydroxylamine

HAS was assumed to be preheated to liquid phase before entering R-2, which meant no solid conveyor system was needed in the model.

Complete S-1 separation

All HAS and initial by-products were assumed to be completely separated at S-1 (stream 12). A simplification, but it kept the model tractable.

Palladium catalyst mass

R-1 was modeled with 35 kg of palladium catalyst at a particle density of 1.2 g/cm³, which is in line with Pd-on-carbon literature values.

Unified CSTR kinetics

Solid kinetic data was hard to come by for all the CSTR reactions, so the same parameters were used across the board. Getting accurate per-reactor kinetics is on the list for next steps.

H₂ flow adjustment

Hydrogen feed was bumped from 2,650 to 2,651 kg/hr in the simulation, just a small rounding tweak to keep the mass balance from drifting.

Errors & Learnings

Challenges encountered in Aspen

!

Negative reactant flow rates in R-3

When the Beckmann rearrangement kinetics were first plugged into the CSTRs, Aspen spat out negative flow rates for the limiting reactant, which is obviously not physically possible. The workaround was to grab kinetic parameters from a CSTR that was already converging and apply those same values to R-3-1 through R-3-3.

!

Bond structure errors for uncommon compounds

Aspen's default bond structure settings for some of the less common compounds (hydroxylamine sulfate, cyclohexanone oxime) were causing errors. Bond angles had to be manually calculated and the structures redrawn in the materials section to get the thermodynamic calculations to work.

!

Distillation column — total vs. partial condenser

T-1 was initially set up as a "total condenser," which was giving two liquid streams out the top and bottom. That's not how a distillation column works, so switching to a "partial condenser" fixed it and gave the expected vapor overhead and liquid bottoms.

!

Oleum chemistry crashing Aspen

Fuming sulfuric acid chemistry involves H₂SO₄ + SO₃ reacting with water to form H₃O⁺, which drives the Beckmann rearrangement. Putting all of those reactions in one reactor caused Aspen to crash every time. Splitting them across three CSTRs in series finally got things running and producing the right flowrates.
