On the 10–20% Result

A 10–20% accuracy on a 9-class problem sits just above chance (11%). The CNN is finding something, but whatever it is learning is too weak, too noisy, or poorly represented by the current feature set. Three factors are most likely responsible. First, class imbalance biases the network toward predicting movement classes since it sees vastly more of them during training. Second, the baseline segments are 56 seconds long while movement segments are approximately 4 seconds — this fundamental structural difference means the CNN is comparing apples to oranges. Third, the accidental overlap between training and test sets means the earlier multi-class accuracy numbers cannot be trusted at all and need to be regenerated from scratch with a proper split.

What the Pipeline Gets Right

The data preparation work — parsing event files, mapping run numbers to task types, segmenting, sorting into classification folders, and converting formats — is solid and fully reproducible from the scripts in this repository. The binary classifier reaching 70–80% is a legitimate signal. Eyes-open vs. eyes-closed EEG has well-understood neurophysiological signatures: alpha suppression (~8–13 Hz) during eyes-open and strong posterior alpha during eyes-closed. The CNN is capturing this distinction reliably, which validates the preprocessing pipeline end-to-end.

The topographic visualization pipeline also works correctly, producing interpretable per-band power maps across all movement conditions. These maps are a useful exploratory tool even if they are not yet integrated into the CNN training loop.

Broader Context

Motor imagery EEG classification is a genuinely hard problem. State-of-the-art results on this specific PhysioNet BCI2000 benchmark typically reach 60–80% on multi-class problems, and those results come from papers with careful subject-specific preprocessing, artifact rejection, and tuned architectures. The current pipeline applies none of those refinements — there is substantial headroom to improve before concluding the approach is fundamentally flawed.

If the multi-class accuracy can be pushed to 80–90% consistently, the downstream applications are meaningful. VR kinesiological simulation and robotic control through imagined motor commands decoded from non-invasive EEG would represent a significant step toward practical brain-machine interfaces.
